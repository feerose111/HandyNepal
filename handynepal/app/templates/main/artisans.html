Supporting Artisan Communities

At HandyNepal, we're committed to supporting artisan communities across Nepal. Through fair trade practices and direct partnerships, we ensure that our artisans receive fair compensation for their work.

A portion of every purchase goes directly to community development initiatives, including education programs for artisans' children and healthcare services in remote villages.

Ram Bahadur

Specialty: Wood Carving Expert

Location: Bhaktapur, Nepal

Experience: 25+ years

Products: Wooden Statues, Masks, Decorative Items

Artisan Story

Ram Bahadur comes from a long line of wood carvers in Bhaktapur, a city renowned for its wood carving tradition. He began learning the craft at the age of 10 from his father and grandfather, who were master carvers in their community.

Specializing in traditional Nepalese motifs and religious figures, Ram's work is characterized by intricate details and smooth finishing. Each piece takes anywhere from a few days to several months to complete, depending on the complexity.

Ram is particularly known for his Buddha statues and traditional Newari window frames, which showcase the rich cultural heritage of Nepal. He uses sustainable local wood and traditional hand tools that have been passed down through generations.

Today, Ram not only creates masterpieces but also trains young apprentices, ensuring that this ancient craft continues to thrive in modern times.

Sita Sharma

Specialty: Textile Weaver

Location: Kirtipur, Nepal

Experience: 18 years

Products: Pashmina Shawls, Traditional Fabrics, Garments

Artisan Story

Sita Sharma learned the art of weaving from her mother at a young age in their small village in Kirtipur. What started as a household necessity became her passion and eventually her livelihood.

Sita specializes in traditional Nepalese textile patterns, particularly the intricate designs found in Dhaka fabric and Pashmina shawls. She uses natural dyes derived from local plants and minerals, creating vibrant colors that are both beautiful and environmentally friendly.

Her weaving process is entirely done by hand on traditional looms, a technique that has remained unchanged for centuries. Each piece takes several weeks to complete, with patterns that tell stories of Nepalese culture and history.

Sita now leads a women's weaving cooperative that provides sustainable income for over 20 women in her community, empowering them through the preservation of traditional crafts.
